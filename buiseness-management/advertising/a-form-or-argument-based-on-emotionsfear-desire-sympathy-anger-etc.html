A form or argument based on emotions:fear, desire, sympathy, anger, etc.

(A) Ethos

(B) ** Pathos

(C) Logos

EXPLANATIONS BELOW

Concept note-1: -The final appeal, pathos, is known as the emotional appeal because it focuses on persuading the audience on the basis of their emotions. When using this appeal, the intent is to connect to the audience’s feelings-whether fear, anger, happiness, sympathy, etc.

Concept note-2: -Pathos, or the appeal to emotion, means to persuade an audience by purposely evoking certain emotions to make them feel the way the author wants them to feel.

Concept note-3: -Using pathos is a very common tactic in argument or persuasion. Appealing to emotion can be a very effective means of sharing your frame of mind and persuading your audience to agree with your point of view. Examples of pathos in persuasion or debate include: Opinion pieces.

Concept note-4: -There are two kinds of pathos: emotional substance and medium mood control. The speaker or writer uses emotional substance when she is trying to elicit an emotional response from her audience.

Concept note-5: -Aristotle taught that a speaker’s ability to persuade an audience is based on how well the speaker appeals to that audience in three different areas: logos, ethos, and pathos. Considered together, these appeals form what later rhetoricians have called the rhetorical triangle.
